I wrote the last question in the 2023 NAC, called Finding Friends. The premise of the question is that there is an astronomer named Andrew trying to chase a star χ\chiχ, and the task is to compute some interesting metrics related to whether or not he will ever be able to catch χ\chiχ.

The original problem was very long, and the final version that ended up being in the exam was condensed by a few parts in order to not make the exam too long or impossible. I figure there is no better place to dump the entire question than my blog, so here is the entire question and solution that I originally submitted.

Question: Finding Friends (solution)

In the future, Earth is perfectly spherical and completely covered with water. Sailor Andrew is on a boat initially positioned at the equator in such that a way that γ\gammaγ, the vernal equinox, is directly overhead. He observes a star χ\chiχ with right ascension and declination (α,δ)=(15∘,10∘)(\alpha, \delta)=(15^{\circ}, 10^{\circ})(α,δ)=(15∘,10∘). He wants to chase χ\chiχ, and he needs your help.

You may assume the following numerical values for each constant:

  • R⊕R_{\oplus}R⊕​ is Earth’s radius (same as the current day value).
  • ω=2π/(24 hrs)\omega = 2\pi/(24\text{ hrs})ω=2π/(24 hrs) is Earth’s angular frequency.
  • His boat is very fast; its maximum speed is ∣v∣=ωR⊕/4\vert v\vert = \omega R_{\oplus}/4∣v∣=ωR⊕​/4.
  • The vernal equinox γ\gammaγ has celestial coordinates (0∘,0∘)(0^{\circ}, 0^{\circ})(0∘,0∘).
  • (α,δ)=(15∘,10∘)(\alpha, \delta) = (15^{\circ}, 10^{\circ})(α,δ)=(15∘,10∘).

Section 1

(a): 2 points (sol)

Calculate the angular distance between γ\gammaγ and χ\chiχ. Express your answer in degrees.

In his starting position, χ\chiχ is somewhere above his horizon. As he sails to different parts of the planet and time passes, χ\chiχ moves around his night sky. He wants sail his boat in order to get χ\chiχ as close to his zenith as possible. To make sure he is as efficient as possible, he wants to make a few calculations first.

(b): 2 points (sol)

If he does not move his boat from his starting position, calculate the minimum zenith distance that χ\chiχ reaches. Express your answer in degrees.

Now, he considers what would happen if he sails straight north at all times.

(c): 3 points (sol)

Starting from his intial position when γ\gammaγ is overhead, he directs his boat straight north and starts sailing with speed ∣v∣\vert v\vert∣v∣. Does χ\chiχ first cross his north-south meridian or east-west meridian? Briefly explain why this is the case.

(d): 3 points (sol)

Calculate the zenith distance of χ\chiχ when it first crosses his north-south meridian. Express your answer in degrees.

(e): 15 points (sol)

Calculate the zenith distance of χ\chiχ when it first crosses his east-west meridian. Express your answer in degrees.

Let XXX be the point in his trajectory (in the celestial sphere) at which χ\chiχ reaches its minimum zenith distance, and NNN be the north celestial pole. Note that XXX is a point in the celestial sphere, and not Earth.

(f): 6 points (sol)

Argue that ∠NγX+∠χXN≈π/2\angle N\gamma X+\angle \chi XN \approx \pi/2∠NγX+∠χXN≈π/2, where both angles are spherical angles. Then, calculate ∠χXN\angle \chi XN∠χXN. Express your answer in degrees.

Note: For this part only, you may assume that the declination of XXX is small, hence the approximation.

(g): 15 points (sol)

Calculate the minimum zenith distance that χ\chiχ reaches. Express your answer in degrees.

For this problem:

  • You may no longer assume that the declination of XXX is small. However, you may use the value of ∠χXN\angle{\chi XN}∠χXN derived in part (f).
  • You are given that the right ascension of XXX is within (α±2∘)(\alpha\pm 2^{\circ})(α±2∘).
  • His trajectory in the celestial sphere is not necessarily a great circle. Be careful when computing angles and lengths!

His next consideration is to see what happens if he steers his ship directly towards χ\chiχ at all times. As before, assume he starts from his initial position with γ\gammaγ overhead, and his boat always moves with speed ∣v∣\vert v\vert∣v∣. If χ\chiχ passes below his horizon, he stops his ship.

As before, assume he starts from his initial position when γ\gammaγ is overhead. Provide a brief argument for why this steering strategy will take him at least one day (i.e., 2π/ω2\pi/\omega2π/ω) until he can get χ\chiχ directly overhead using this method.

Let ζ\zetaζ be the counterclockwise angle from vrv_{r}vr​ to vvv. Let θ\thetaθ be the counterclockwise angle from vrv_{r}vr​ to vtv_tvt​, where vtv_tvt​ is his net velocity in the celestial sphere.

Now we will attempt to characterize how the zenith distance changes over his trajectory. At any particular point in his path, let ϕ\phiϕ be his latitude.

(h): 2 points (sol)

Find an expression for tan⁡θ\tan \thetatanθ in terms of ζ\zetaζ, vvv, ω\omegaω, R⊕R_{\oplus}R⊕​, and ϕ\phiϕ. Some of these constants have known numerical values, but you do not have to substitute them in.

(Hint: Consider the components of vtv_tvt​. Your final expression should involve all the listed constants.)

In his current trajectory, prove that a small change in the zenith distance of χ\chiχ, Δz\Delta zΔz, corresponds to a horizontal displacement in the celestial sphere by an angle of

Δλ=∣Δz∣cos⁡θcos⁡(ζ−θ)cos⁡ϕ.\Delta \lambda = \frac{\vert \Delta z\vert \cos{\theta}}{\cos{(\zeta - \theta)}\cos \phi}.Δλ=cos(ζ−θ)cosϕ∣Δz∣cosθ​.

Note that Δz\Delta zΔz and Δλ\Delta \lambdaΔλ are small angular changes.

Section 3

He is not satisfied with these results. He feels there should be an optimal way to get χ\chiχ directly overhead, as fast as possible. Help him figure out the fastest way to get χ\chiχ to his zenith.

(k): 2 points (sol)

Given that he is allowed to maneuver his boat however he wants throughout the duration of his journey, and that he starts from his initial position with γ\gammaγ overhead, show that χ\chiχ cannot be at his zenith the first time it culminates, i.e., crosses his north-south meridian.

(l): 7 points (sol)

Let ttt be the fastest time he can get χ\chiχ to his zenith. Compute the quantity (t−20 hrs)(t-\text{20 hrs})(t−20 hrs), in seconds.

Solution: Finding Friends (question)

Section 1

(a) (ques)

Let NNN be the north celestial pole and ddd be our desired distance. Then, the spherical law of cosines in △Nγχ\triangle{N\gamma\chi}△Nγχ gives

cos⁡d=cos⁡90∘cos⁡(90∘−δ)+sin⁡90∘sin⁡(90∘−δ)cos⁡α,\cos{d} = \cos{90^{\circ}}\cos{(90^{\circ}-\delta)} + \sin{90^{\circ}}\sin{(90^{\circ}-\delta)}\cos{\alpha},cosd=cos90∘cos(90∘−δ)+sin90∘sin(90∘−δ)cosα,

which implies d=arccos⁡(cos⁡δcos⁡α)≈17.96∘d = \arccos{(\cos{\delta}\cos{\alpha})}\approx 17.96^{\circ}d=arccos(cosδcosα)≈17.96∘.

(b) (ques)

Since χ\chiχ is to his east, it has not yet culminated. Also, since he is not moving, χ\chiχ achieves its minimum zenith distance as it culminates.

One way to figure out this minimum zenith distance is to consider the view from his horizon. χ\chiχ moves through the small circle representing the declination line at δ\deltaδ. Since he is located on the equator, the celestial equator from his perspective passes through the zenith. Therefore, the minimum zenith distance is the distance between the declination line at δ\deltaδ, and the celestial equator, which is δ=10∘\delta = 10^{\circ}δ=10∘.

An alternative approach is to consider his movement through the celestial sphere. Culmination occurs when his location and χ\chiχ have the same right ascension. At this point, their distance is δ\deltaδ, so this is the minimum zenith distance.

(c) (ques)

χ\chiχ starts in the northeastern quadrant of the sky. When χ\chiχ crosses the north-south meridian, it changes from the east to west hemisphere. When χ\chiχ crosses the the east-west meridian, it changes from the north to south hemisphere.

At time ttt, his location in the celestial sphere is given by (ωt,ωt/4)(\omega t, \omega t/4)(ωt,ωt/4), so (α,α/4)(\alpha, \alpha/4)(α,α/4) is a point in his trajectory. Since α/4<δ\alpha/4 < \deltaα/4<δ, χ\chiχ culminates in his northern sky. Thus, χ\chiχ passes the north-south meridian before passing the east-west meridian.

(d) (ques)

χ\chiχ culminates when it has the same right ascension as Andrew, so ωt=α\omega t = \alphaωt=α. So, the zenith distance is given by

δ−α4≈6.25∘.\delta - \frac{\alpha}{4} \approx 6.25^{\circ}.δ−4α​≈6.25∘.

(e) (ques)

Let DDD be his position in the celestial sphere. When χ\chiχ first crosses the east-west meridian, DDD must satisfy ∠NDχ=90∘\angle ND\chi = 90^{\circ}∠NDχ=90∘, as shown in the diagram below. The red line in the diagram represents his trajectory in the celestial sphere (this trajectory is not a great circle, so we cannot use it in our calculations).

Now, chasing angles:

  • The perpendicular condition implies that DDD is the apex of another great circle (call Γ\GammaΓ) that passes through both DDD and χ\chiχ. Γ\GammaΓ intersects the equator at a point AAA, such that DA=90∘DA = 90^{\circ}DA=90∘.
  • As labelled in the diagram, let BBB and CCC be the feet of χ\chiχ and DDD from NNN, respectively (so that ∠χBA=∠DCA=90∘\angle \chi BA = \angle DCA = 90^{\circ}∠χBA=∠DCA=90∘). Since DDD is the apex of Γ\GammaΓ, DA=CA=90∘DA = CA = 90^{\circ}DA=CA=90∘.
  • We know γB=α\gamma B = \alphaγB=α and γC=ωt\gamma C = \omega tγC=ωt, so AB=90∘+α−ωtAB = 90^{\circ}+\alpha-\omega tAB=90∘+α−ωt.
  • We also know that χB=δ\chi B = \deltaχB=δ, and DC=ωt/4DC = \omega t/4DC=ωt/4.

Our goal is to find the zenith distance, which is χD\chi DχD. To find this arc, we’ll need ωt\omega tωt, which will let us solve for the right ascension of DDD. Let ε=∠χAB=∠DAC\varepsilon = \angle{\chi AB} = \angle{DAC}ε=∠χAB=∠DAC. The four-parts formula in both △χAB\triangle{\chi AB}△χAB and △DAC\triangle{DAC}△DAC gives

tan⁡ε=tan⁡χBsin⁡AB=tan⁡DCsin⁡AC.\tan{\varepsilon} = \frac{\tan \chi B}{\sin AB} = \frac{\tan DC}{\sin AC}.tanε=sinABtanχB​=sinACtanDC​.

Substituting known values, this reduces to the equation

tan⁡δ=cos⁡(ωt−α)tan⁡(ωt4)  ⟹  ωt=4arctan⁡(tan⁡δcos⁡(ωt−α)).\tan \delta = \cos{(\omega t - \alpha)}\tan{\left(\frac{\omega t}{4}\right)}\implies \omega t = 4\arctan \left(\frac{\tan \delta}{\cos(\omega t - \alpha)}\right).tanδ=cos(ωt−α)tan(4ωt​)⟹ωt=4arctan(cos(ωt−α)tanδ​).

We can use this to numerically iterate for ωt\omega tωt. Our initial guess does not need to be good; any value from around 10∘10^{\circ}10∘ to 70∘70^{\circ}70∘ converges in a few iterations, giving us ωt≈46.97∘\omega t\approx 46.97^{\circ}ωt≈46.97∘.

Finally, a few more angles:

  • ∠χND=∠BNC=ωt−α\angle \chi ND = \angle BNC = \omega t - \alpha∠χND=∠BNC=ωt−α.
  • Nχ=90∘−δN\chi = 90^{\circ}-\deltaNχ=90∘−δ.

χD=arccos⁡(sin⁡δsin⁡(ωt4)+cos⁡(ωt−α)cos⁡δcos⁡(ωt4))≈31.42∘.\chi D = \arccos{\left(\sin \delta \sin \left(\frac{\omega t}{4}\right) + \cos{(\omega t-\alpha)}\cos \delta \cos{\left(\frac{\omega t}{4}\right)}\right)}\approx 31.42^{\circ}.χD=arccos(sinδsin(4ωt​)+cos(ωt−α)cosδcos(4ωt​))≈31.42∘.

(f) (ques)

The zenith distance χX\chi XχX is minimized when ∠χXγ=90∘\angle \chi X\gamma = 90^{\circ}∠χXγ=90∘. Let X′X'X′ be the foot of XXX from NNN, so that ∠NX′γ=90∘\angle NX'\gamma = 90^{\circ}∠NX′γ=90∘. Then, if we let β=∠NXχ\beta = \angle NX\chiβ=∠NXχ:

  • Since the latitude of XXX is small, and △XX′γ\triangle{XX'\gamma}△XX′γ is right, we can say that ∠γXX′+∠XγX′≈90∘\angle \gamma XX' + \angle X\gamma X'\approx 90^{\circ}∠γXX′+∠XγX′≈90∘, so ∠XγX′≈β\angle X\gamma X' \approx \beta∠XγX′≈β.   • Since ∠NγX′=90∘\angle N\gamma X' = 90^{\circ}∠NγX′=90∘, β\betaβ and ∠NγX\angle N\gamma X∠NγX add to 90∘90^{\circ}90∘, as desired.

To compute β\betaβ, we can use our approximation of △XX′γ\triangle{XX'\gamma}△XX′γ as a flat right triangle to say that tan⁡β≈1/4\tan{\beta}\approx 1/4tanβ≈1/4, so β≈14∘\beta\approx 14^{\circ}β≈14∘.

(g) (ques)

Consider △NXχ\triangle{NX\chi}△NXχ. We know the lengths of two sides, Nχ=90∘−δN\chi = 90^{\circ}-\deltaNχ=90∘−δ and NX=90∘−ωt/4NX = 90^{\circ}-\omega t/4NX=90∘−ωt/4, and the values of a nested and adjacent angle, ∠N=ωt−α\angle N = \omega t-\alpha∠N=ωt−α and ∠X=β\angle X = \beta∠X=β. We want to find the length of the zenith angle, which is XχX\chiXχ. Unfortunately, we can’t directly solve for XχX\chiXχ, but we may first try solving for ωt\omega tωt using the information we already have, which will give us enough information about the triangle to solve for XχX\chiXχ.

Applying the four parts formula:

sin⁡(ωt4)cos⁡(ωt−α)=cos⁡(ωt4)cot⁡(90∘−δ)−sin⁡(ωt−α)cot⁡β.\sin{\left(\frac{\omega t}{4}\right)}\cos{(\omega t - \alpha)} = \cos{\left(\frac{\omega t}{4}\right)}\cot{(90^{\circ}-\delta)} - \sin{(\omega t - \alpha)}\cot{\beta}.sin(4ωt​)cos(ωt−α)=cos(4ωt​)cot(90∘−δ)−sin(ωt−α)cotβ.

As in part (e), we can isolate ωt\omega tωt and try to solve this equation with numerical iteration:

ωt=4arctan⁡(tan⁡δcos⁡(ωt−α)−sin⁡(ωt−α)cot⁡βcos⁡(ωt/4)cos⁡(ωt−α)).\omega t = 4\arctan{\left(\frac{\tan \delta}{\cos{(\omega t-\alpha)}} - \frac{\sin{(\omega t- \alpha)}\cot \beta}{\cos{(\omega t/4)}\cos{(\omega t - \alpha)}}\right)}.ωt=4arctan(cos(ωt−α)tanδ​−cos(ωt/4)cos(ωt−α)sin(ωt−α)cotβ​).

Finally, the law of sines in △XχN\triangle{X\chi N}△XχN gives

(h) (ques)

The vertical component of vtv_tvt​ is given by vsin⁡ζv\sin{\zeta}vsinζ, while the horizontal component is given by vcos⁡ζ+ωR⊕cos⁡ϕv\cos{\zeta} + \omega R_{\oplus}\cos{\phi}vcosζ+ωR⊕​cosϕ. Therefore,

(k) (ques)

In order for χ\chiχ to be directly overhead, he needs to have the same declination and right ascension as χ\chiχ.

The amount of time he needs to increase his latitude to δ\deltaδ is bounded below by
